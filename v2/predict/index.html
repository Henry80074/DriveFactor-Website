Seasonal patterns

December rural roads are 40–60% more dangerous per vehicle-hour than the annual average. Summer afternoons on motorways are below average.

🌧️

Precipitation modelling

Eight precipitation bins from dry to extreme (0–11+ mm/h), each validated against crash rate uplift by road type. Light drizzle on motorways behaves differently from heavy rain on B-roads.

💨

Wind gust intelligence

Seven gust bins from calm to storm. High-sided vehicle risk on exposed A-roads in gale conditions is captured separately from general flow disruption.

🛣️

Road type sensitivity

Five road types — motorway, rural A, urban A, rural B, urban B. Each has a distinct temporal risk profile. Urban A-roads in evening peak behave very differently from rural motorways.

📊

AADF-weighted exposure

Crash rates are normalised by vehicle-hour exposure using AADF counts — the model measures crash risk per vehicle, not raw crash counts that would bias toward busy roads.

🌩️

Combined condition scoring

Rain, wind, hour, month, day type, and road type interact in a single GAM. The combined score correctly captures, for example, a wet December Monday evening on a rural A-road.

Environmental Hazard

Sun Glare
Risk Intelligence

Sun glare is a significant but invisible risk factor — entirely absent from standard crash maps and risk models. The DriveFactor sun glare layer combines solar trajectory modelling with road orientation data to identify which specific road segments face directly into the sun during morning or evening rush hours.

The effect is highly localised and seasonal — a 400m section of road can be severely glare-affected in October at 07:30 while an adjacent parallel road sees no glare at all. Standard temporal models miss this entirely.
